Our Mission

Our mission in the development of this site was to create a safe space for teenagers to have access to mental health awareness, support, and nearby accessibility. We wanted to also develop a chatbot to serve as an affordable version of customizable therapy available with premium features for small fees with varying time frames. By providing accessibility help and availability to a support network filled with advice and awareness from teens to teens, we create a safe and comforting environment where young adults struggling with or unaware of mental health can find resources and a network of support to help find their best selves.

Our Approach

  • Our main goal of the website was to make mental health awareness not only available, but accessible and affordable. Our marketing approach is to offer our chatbot therapy service with premium subscription packages available for communication customized by your own personal therapist tailored to you.
  • In our society, although mental health is becoming a much more prevalent and important topic, most parents and caregivers of young children and teens do not believe in therapy, medical prescriptions, or other serious treatments for mental health. Especially since most serious and successful therapists charge somewhere between $200 and $800 dollars on average, and most families either cannot afford their prices or refuse to pay for someone to ‘tell their child they are sad’.

  • We also will be collaborating with other mental health resources and companies through ad services and article references.

  • Just like people coping with depression, you as well have to stay connected with people you love and places you love as well. Hang out with people that make you feel safe and are always there for what you need.

  • If you are going through a situation where you feel anxious and start thinking negative, remember that at the end of the day everything is going to be okay and that what is happening in your life now is not going to be the same in the next 3 months.

These are some of our tips that we want to give out to you and that we feel will be the most useful to you. Of course, if you would like to learn more, check our our school advice page!

Eating Disorders

Dealing with ones image is something that is always a bit unsettling, especially during these years when we feel quite self conscious. We have felt it too, and here are our tips:

  1. Assemble your team of supporters/healthcare professionals. This includes your primary physician (and a referral), school counselors (if you are comfortable with them), friends, family, etc)
  2. Find healthier ways to cope with negative emotions. Try to identify what you are really feeling and see how that affects your eating habits. Suggestions from us include hitting up a friend, listening to music, reading, going out for a walk, etc
  3. Develop a good relationship with food! Do not be too rigid with rules, and allow yourself flexibility. Dieting should not be in mind as it will make you preoccupied and even obsessed with it. Attempt to eat every three hours and plan ahead with snacks and drinks, and remember to listen to your body when it is asking for food!
  4. Learn how to accept and love yourself! Make a list of all your best positive qualities, stop body checking, avoid fat talk, and most importantly, challenge negative self talk!

Bipolar Disorder

Although it is not entirely known exactly what causes one to have bipolar disorder, there are definitely factors that play a role in the onset and progression of this disorder, which include genetic, neurochemical, and environmental factors. Currently it is thought that there is a neurobiological disorder that occurs in the brain with malfunctions of the brain chemicals serotonin, dopamine, and noradrenaline.

Here are some tips/advice for coping with Bipolar disorder and links to more resources to help you!

  • If you are not doing so already, it is DEFINITELY a good idea to seek help from a professional, such as your doctor, therapists, etc to seek medication which could help with symptoms as well as to simply have someone to talk to!
  • Monitor and track your symptoms and moods. If you notice anything obvious changing in your mood, it might be too late to intercept, so look out for any subtle changes or things that trigger a reaction out of you. If it helps, make a list early symptoms that have previously caused mood episodes.
  • Develop a daily routine that you can follow to get symptoms under control and to keep it at bay.
  • It would be a good idea to figure out how to find ways to exercise daily (aerobic exercise works great, and this includes running, swimming, dancing, climbing, and even walking) ideally for 30 minutes a day (spreading it out b/w the day works as well)and to avoid sitting for long periods of time
  • Keep a strict sleep schedule! Not getting enough sleep can trigger an episode, so consistency is key!

Additional tips are to:

  1. Avoid/minimize napping that will prevent you from sleeping at night
  2. Avoid electronics before heading off to bed and instead try taking a bath, reading a book, or listening to relaxing music
  3. Reduce caffeine intake after lunch and alcohol at night as they interfere with your sleep
  4. Reach out to people for connection! It is important to not isolate yourself when it is support and love you will really need to get ahead!
  5. Reach out and connect with supportive friends and family, which is a really therapeutic experience.
  6. Find a support group with others who are dealing with the same thing! You will feel understood, get support, and perhaps find tips/advice you have not heard before

OCD (Obsessive Compulsive Disorder)

It is a terrible feeling to have compulsive behaviors and obsessive thoughts in your daily life, but we can help with managing and improving you daily life!

  1. Find your triggers! Record a list of the triggers in your daily life, and the obsessions they provoke.
  2. Learn to resist OCD compulsions, as the more you try to avoid them, the scarier they seem. By repeatedly exposing yourself to triggers, you can learn to prevent compulsive behaviors, and medically this is known as exposure and response prevention.
  3. Manage stress, because although it does not directly cause OCD, it can trigger symptoms or make them worse. Physical exercise and connecting with others in person are also very effective ways to calm your nervous system.
  4. Make lifestyle changes, such as getting 30 minutes of aerobic activity daily, getting enough sleep, and avoiding alcohol and nicotine.

Freshman Year

Freshman year is tough, we have definetly been there. But we got through it and so can you! As much as we all like to hope and wish that freshman year of high school will be exactly like all the movies and TV shows and you will trip over the lunch tray and start falling before your future boyfriend/girlfriend catches you and falls in love, unfortunately that could not be farther from the reality of high school. Hopefully with our tips, freshman year will be as great as it can be and will set you up for 3 more successful and happy years!

Our Tried and Tested Tips Social Advice Time Management School Supplies List

Our Best Freshman Year Tips

  • Come in with an open mind. Try not to have expectations for what this year and your high school experience will be like.
  • Be prepared for an academic challenge. The transition from middle school to high school is not an easy one so remember that it is okay if the work does not come naturally right from the get go. Relax and embrace the challenge knowing you will get used to it!
  • Be yourself. Try to remember that your true friends are going to want to be friends with YOU, not what you think they want you to be. High school will be a much more positive experience if you let your teachers get to know the real you and do not try to put on a show.
  • Do not succumb to peer pressure. We know its hard, but this goes hand in hand with being yourself. Never do something you do not want to do or something you will regret later. No this does not apply to staying out one minute past your curfew to get ice cream, but you know what we are talking about ;).
  • Be social. Freshman year is less about your schoolwork and more about discovering your interests, finding your friends, and finding yourself and where you fit into your high schools community.

Social Advice!

  • Be honest yet modest. High school is hard because even though you might have had friends who you went to middle school with, you still want to branch out and meet new people. Obviously it is difficult to just jump right in to a new friendship, so when you are talking to new people and getting to know each other, remember to tell stories in a way that does not come off as bragging or self-centered. Be yourself but remember these people do not know you and you do not know them so try not to come on too strong.
  • Embrace who you are. Some people may just not connect with you and you might feel the urge to change who you are so they will like you, but as hard as it is to admit, some people just will not like you in life. There is no need to not like them in return for it or hold them against them; just because they do not want to be best friends does not mean that you cannot be the bigger person and be civil.
  • Stand up for others, they will remember. We hate that it still exists, but bullying will never not be an issue at any school and we always need to advocate for those who cannot stand up for themselves. They may not be your best friends, but the worst feeling in high school is knowing that someone is getting hurt and you did nothing to stop it when you easily could have. Being a friend to everyone is the best way to start your high school career.

Time Management

  • Prioritize. Do not get overwhelmed, start by making a list of everything you have to do each night and label each task by priority. Either do the easiest tasks first or do the assignments for your morning classes first, or really any strategy that works for you, and slowly check things off your list. Do not forget to take breaks and reward yourself at the end of the day!
  • Do not overbook yourself. You are just beginning high school, you want to join as many clubs and teams as possible while still making friends and hanging out with people, spending time with your family, and focusing on your schoolwork. Unfortunately you will not be able to do it all. Pick three or four of the extracurriculars you are most passionate or excited about and go all in on those.
  • Do not multitask. It seems obvious but trying to start and finish multiple tasks at the same time just means you will not be able to give either task your best effort and it will just make you feel more overwhelmed and not in control. The best thing you can do for yourself is to take everything one step at a time and give it your best effort.
  • Take advantage of free periods and mornings. The most important lesson everyone will always tell you about high school is to get sleep. And the most frustrating piece of advice high schoolers get is to get sleep, but as much as we all hate hearing it because it feels impossible, it really is true, sleep is critical. The number one way I get as much sleep as I can is by using my free periods and my time before school to get work done. If it is 12 at night and I still have three more assignments to get to, I know that they will not be done correctly or well if I do them now, but if I start them in the morning and finish them during my morning frees when I am more productive, my work will be correct and complete.

How to Get Yourself College Prep Ready

College. That big nightmare that’s looming over your head that you are currently are pretending does not and never will exist or affect you. Well, time to face the music my friend. Here are some tips to make the reality a little less harsh.

  • Make two lists. One listing all of your interests, passions, talents, or things you just enjoy to do; and another on dream qualities or characteristics you would love to have at your college. It can be as crazy as an infinity pool with a cotton candy bar in the bathroom, or waterbeds in the dorm, but try to be more realistic. Think about questions like 'Do I want a gym on campus where I can go to work out even if I am not part of a sports team?', 'What kind of dining hall would work for me and my dietary needs?', 'Would I be able to have a single or even a four person dorm room if I wanted one?', 'Do I want a school where I have to commit to a major during the application process?'
  • Take a practice SAT and ACT. This is going to be the worst part of the college process; but the earlier you figure out which test is right for you, the better your late sophomore and early junior year will be.
  • Start asking people who know you what they think a good school environment would be for you. If you are as frozen in anxiety as I was during the beginning of my college process, you have no idea what you want or how to even say the word college. Well in that case, the best thing you can do is ask for help. Ask your family what region of the world they think is a good fit for you, ask your college counselor if they think a small liberal arts or a big university seems more right for you. Do not ask questions they cannot answer like 'what should I major in?'', but start to get a feel for where you should start looking by asking the people who know you the best.

How to Have a Social Life and Maintain Your Grades

Now for every high schoolers biggest dilemma: how to have a life and still save your grades from plummeting like the Titanic. Well we do not even really know ourselves, but here is how we try:

  • Dedicate at least 30 minutes a day to yourself to do whatever you feel like you need to do that day. It could be something as easy as take a nap, have a bath, stretch, eat a whole bag of skittles, use the time to really indulge in self-love.
  • Create a routine that works for you and stick to it, even on the weekends ;). Pick a time to wake up every morning, a time to go to bed around every night, times where you get a snack, days where you watch a TV show or times to spend with your family.
  • Find the times and places where you are most productive, most unproductive, and most relaxed and remember them, either keep them in your phone or write them down; and in those times where you went out with a friend only to come home to a pile of homework due tomorrow, you will thank us.

The Common Application

The most well-known way of applying to college. We know it seems daunting and it looks like a digital version of the stress looming over your brain, but if you take it step by step, we can all get done.

  • Start early and give yourself time for each section. The common app is the bulk of your application for most colleges along with essays and supplemental writings, and there are multiple parts. So the earlier you register and knock off the small parts, the smoother the whole process will be.
  • Make a game plan.Plan out what part of the common app you can do when and who you need to talk to in order to fill in any gaps. Make a list of your extracurriculars for your activities section. Think through your timing and prioritize your sections to get it done fast and well.
  • Pick and choose your strongest activities.Remember you only have 10 activities slots available, so even if you have done 30 or 40 extracurriculars, pick the ones that best represent you and that you are proud of. You want your application to reflect you in as many different lights as you can.

Tips from College Counselors to Rising Seniors

  • Do not the adults in your life take over your process.Remember that YOU are the only one applying to college. Your parents and family are there to support you, but remind them that this is your process, and you need to be in the drivers seat.
  • Try not to fall in love.If you have a one-track mind on your top college, you will neglect to see all the amazing colleges around you, and on the off chance you do not get accepted to that one school, it will be hard to pick yourself back up and get that excite about the schools you do get into. Be excited about every school you apply to, and if you do not have butterflies when you hit the apply button to a college, it might not be the one.
  • Deadlines are deadlines, so treat them like deadlines. Applications are serious, and if you forget to hit the send button, there is no extention waiting for you on the other side. You have one shot and one only. If you know yourself and know that you are not good with deadlines, mark the appliation deadlines as a few days before the real deadlines and have your family be set on that date as well, giving you a security blanket if you forget to send it in.
  • Never forget your manners.Emails and interviews are some of the first impressions college admission counselors will have of you. Always have a counselor or a parent grammar check your emails and prepare for interviews. They may not be a top priority, but they are important to forge good connections.

Essays

  • Try not to overthink it.Although the common app essay and supplements seem like the most important part of your application because they are the only place to show your personality and let the counselors reading your application to see the real you, remember you can only fit so much within the word limit. The best way to get started is to think of the essay as a way to introduce yourself to someone who has never met you and knows nothing about you. What would you tell them about yourself to let them build an impression of you? And go from there.
  • Do not be afraid of being yourself.If you are funny in real life, add some of your humor in your essay! You want to be as truthful and real as you can be, so open yourself up and be yourself.
  • Avoid the sob storiesIt has been said over and over, but stay away from death, divorce, and depression. College admission counselors who are reading your essays have seen the classic pity me story over and over again. The main goal is to be yourself and to be unique. Tell a story that others cannot tell the way you can.
